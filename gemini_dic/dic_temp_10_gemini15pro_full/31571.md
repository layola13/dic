chasidism:/ˈkæsɪdɪzəm/ | n. |哈西德主义| A branch of Orthodox Judaism, founded in 18th-century Eastern Europe by Rabbi Israel ben Eliezer, known as the Baal Shem Tov. It emphasizes piety, spiritual joy, and the importance of direct experience of God. | Example: Chasidism is known for its distinctive dress and customs. 哈西德主义以其独特的服饰和习俗而闻名。| Synonym: Hasidut | Antonym: (No direct antonym, but perhaps Misnagdim, a historical opposing movement) | Noun

ETYMOLOGY->
From Yiddish חסידות‎ (khsides), from Hebrew חֲסִידוּת‎ (khasidut, “piety, devoutness”), from חָסִיד‎ (khasid, “pious person”).
