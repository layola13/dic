ETYMOLOGY->
From Old English *æfter (attested only in compounds), from Proto-Germanic *after (source also of Old Saxon aftar, Old Frisian efter, Old Norse eptir, Dutch achter, German achter, Gothic 𐌰𐍆𐍄𐌰𐍂 (aftar)), from Proto-Indo-European *ap-tero-, comparative form of *apo "off, away from."  The original sense was "following in place or order," hence "behind."
