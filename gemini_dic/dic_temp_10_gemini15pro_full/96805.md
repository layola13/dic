inert gases: /ɪˈnɜːt ˈɡæsɪz/ /ɪˈnɝːt ˈɡæsɪz/
| n. |惰性气体|Gases that do not readily undergo chemical reactions.  They are also known as noble gases.|Helium is an inert gas used in balloons.|Synonyms: noble gases, rare gases|Antonyms: reactive gases
|noun|

inert: /ɪˈnɜːt/ /ɪˈnɝːt/
| adj. |惰性的，迟钝的|Lacking the ability or strength to move or act; chemically inactive.|The inert gas argon is often used in welding.|Synonyms: inactive, passive, sluggish|Antonyms: active, reactive, dynamic
|adjective|

gas: /ɡæs/
| n. |气体，煤气|A substance or matter in a state in which it will expand freely to fill the whole of a container, having no fixed shape (unlike a solid) and no fixed volume (unlike a liquid).|Helium is a lighter-than-air gas.|Synonyms: vapor, fume|Antonyms: solid, liquid
|noun|

词源：
inert: From Latin *iners*, from *in-* (not) + *ars* (skill, art).  The chemical sense arose from the observation that these gases did not readily participate in chemical reactions.
gas: Coined by the 17th-century Flemish chemist J.B. van Helmont, probably from Greek *chaos* (empty space).
