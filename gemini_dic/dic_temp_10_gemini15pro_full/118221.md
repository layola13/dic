mercantilism: /ˈmɜːrkəntɪlɪzəm/ | n. | 重商主义 | An economic theory and practice common in Europe from the 16th to the 18th century that promoted governmental regulation of a nation's economy for the purpose of augmenting state power at the expense of rival national powers. It was characterized by the belief that national strength depended on a favorable balance of trade and the accumulation of precious metals (especially gold and silver).  |  Mercantilism was the dominant economic system in Europe during the age of exploration.  |  近义词: protectionism, statism | 反义词: free trade, laissez-faire | 词性: noun

Etymology: From French *mercantilisme*, from *mercantile* "mercantile," from Italian *mercantile*, from *mercante* "merchant," from Latin *mercans* "merchant," present participle of *mercari* "to trade," from *merx* "merchandise."
