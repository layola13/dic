euphuistic:/juːfjuˈɪstɪk/ | adj. | Euphuistic（尤弗伊斯式的）| Characterized by elaborate, artificial, and highly ornamented language; resembling the style of John Lyly's Euphues.  A style popular in the late 16th century marked by antithesis, alliteration, and frequent similes drawn from mythology and natural history. |  Her writing style was criticized for being too euphuistic, full of obscure allusions and elaborate metaphors.  她的写作风格被批评为过于尤弗伊斯式，充满了晦涩的典故和复杂的比喻。| Synonyms: ornate, flowery, affected, bombastic, high-flown, stilted | Antonyms: plain, simple, natural, unadorned, concise |词性: 形容词

Etymology: From Euphues, the title character of John Lyly's prose romances Euphues: The Anatomy of Wit (1579) and Euphues and His England (1580), + -istic. Ultimately from Greek euphues “well-grown, comely,” from eu- “well” + phue- “growth, form, nature.”
