all-in-the-wind:/ɔːl ɪn ðə wɪnd/| adj.| 无效的，没有结果的，空洞的|  Meaning something is futile, groundless, or without substance, often referring to rumors or plans that are unlikely to come to fruition.  It implies a lack of solidity or foundation.  The image evoked is something being dispersed and lost like the wind. |  All their plans were all-in-the-wind after the funding fell through.  他们所有的计划在资金中断后都落空了。 |Synonyms: baseless, groundless, unsubstantiated, vain, futile, idle, empty| Antonyms: concrete, solid, substantial, valid, well-founded| adjective

Etymology->
The phrase originates from the nautical term "in the wind" referring to a sail flapping uselessly when a ship is head to wind and cannot make progress.  This sense of futility was then extended metaphorically to other situations.
