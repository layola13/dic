paludamentum:/ˌpælʊdəˈmɛntəm/| n. | 军官披风; 将军斗篷 | A military cloak worn by Roman generals and high-ranking officers.  It was a large, rectangular piece of cloth, typically purple or scarlet, fastened on the right shoulder with a clasp or brooch. | Caesar wore his paludamentum as he addressed the troops.  凯撒在向部队讲话时穿着他的披风。|  cloak, mantle, cape | - | noun
Etymology->
From Latin *palūdāmentum*, from *palūdāre* (“to clothe in a *pallium*”) + *-mentum* (suffix forming nouns of instrument or result).  *Pallium* refers to a type of cloak or mantle.
