compromise:/ˈkɒmprəmaɪz/ (UK) /ˈkɑːmprəmaɪz/ (US)| n. vt. vi.|妥协，折衷；危及，损害|A settlement of differences by mutual concessions; an agreement reached by adjustment of conflicting or opposing claims.  To expose or make vulnerable to danger, suspicion, scandal, etc.|例句：They reached a compromise over the terms of the contract. 他们在合同条款上达成了妥协。|近义词：agreement, settlement, bargain, concession|反义词：disagreement, dispute, conflict|词性:名词, 动词

compromit:/ˈkɒmprəmɪt/ (UK) /ˈkɑːmprəmaɪt/ (US)| vt. n.|损害（名誉），连累；妥协，折衷（较少用）|To bring into disrepute or danger by indiscreet, foolish, or reckless behavior; to cause to become vulnerable or subject to danger, suspicion, or disrepute.  An embarrassing or difficult situation.|例句：The scandal compromitted the politician's reputation.  这桩丑闻损害了这位政治家的名誉。|近义词：embarrass, jeopardize, endanger, expose, discredit|反义词：protect, safeguard, enhance|词性:动词, 名词


ETYMOLOGY->
From Late Latin compromittere, from Latin com- ‘together’ + promittere ‘promise’.  Originally meaning a joint promise to abide by an arbiter's decision, it later acquired the sense of making concessions and, ultimately, the negative connotation of bringing into disrepute.
