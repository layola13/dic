mercantilists: /ˈmɜːrkəntɪlɪsts/ | n. | 重商主义者 |  Adherents or advocates of mercantilism, an economic theory prevalent in Europe from the 16th to the 18th centuries that promoted governmental regulation of a nation's economy for the purpose of augmenting state power at the expense of rival national powers.  It was characterized by the belief that national strength could be maximized by limiting imports via tariffs and maximizing exports. | The mercantilists believed that a nation's wealth was best measured by its reserves of gold and silver. | 近义词: protectionists | 反义词: free traders | 词性: noun

ETYMOLOGY->
From French *mercantiliste*, from *mercantile* "merchant," ultimately from Latin *mercari* "to trade," *merx* "merchandise."  The -ist suffix denotes adherence to a doctrine or system.
