doublespeak:/ˈdʌbəlˌspiːk/| n.|双重语言，模棱两可的话|Language deliberately used to disguise, distort, or reverse the meaning of words.  It is most often used by governments and organizations to make unpleasant or controversial activities sound acceptable.|The government's press release was full of doublespeak, making it difficult to understand their true intentions. 政府的新闻稿充满了模棱两可的话，很难理解他们的真实意图。|Synonyms: euphemism, jargon, gobbledygook, ambiguity, equivocation|Antonyms: plain speaking, clarity, directness, transparency|noun


ETYMOLOGY->
From *double* + *speak*, coined by George Orwell in his novel *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (1949), though the concept existed before he named it.  It's inspired by the terms "doublethink" and "newspeak" also from the same novel.
