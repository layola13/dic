epanalepsis:/ˌɛpəˈnælpɪsɪs/ | n. | 重复;首尾呼应 | The repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning and end of a clause, sentence, or paragraph.  It is a rhetorical device used to emphasize a point or create a sense of unity and closure.  | Example: "Alone, he walked alone."  This sentence uses epanalepsis to highlight the character's loneliness. | 近义词：repetition, reiteration | 反义词：None specifically, but the opposite would be avoiding repetition.


USAGE->
This word is a noun and doesn't have verb conjugations like "what".  Its usage is purely descriptive of a rhetorical device.  Examples illustrate its use:

Example 1: "The king is dead, long live the king!"  This uses epanalepsis to create a dramatic effect and emphasize the continuity of power despite the death of the monarch.

Example 2:  "Fear leads to anger. Anger leads to hate. Hate leads to suffering." This example uses epanalepsis in a series of phrases to build momentum and impact. Each sentence starts and ends with the same word, creating a rhythmic and memorable effect.


PHRASE->
Epanalepsis doesn't have common phrases in the same way "what" does.  It's a rhetorical technique that can be applied within phrases or sentences. Examples of sentences employing epanalepsis are provided above.
