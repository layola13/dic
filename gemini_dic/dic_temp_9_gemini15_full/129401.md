nominalism:/ˈnɑːmɪnəlɪzəm/| n. |名义论；唯名论|The philosophical theory that only individual things exist and that general or abstract terms have no objective reference.  Nominalism rejects the existence of universals, arguing that concepts like "redness" or "justice" are merely names we apply to individual things that share certain characteristics.  It contrasts with realism, which posits the independent existence of universals.|Example:  The debate between realism and nominalism raged for centuries in medieval philosophy.|近义词：conceptualism (to a lesser extent)|反义词：realism

词源:

The word "nominalism" derives from the Latin word "nominalis," meaning "relating to names."  The suffix "-ism" indicates a doctrine or system of belief.  Therefore, nominalism literally means "the doctrine of names," reflecting its core focus on the role of names and concepts in our understanding of reality.
