glycosaminoglycans:/ˌɡlaɪkoʊˌsæmɪnoʊˈɡlɪkənz/| n. |糖胺聚糖|Glycosaminoglycans (GAGs) are long, unbranched polysaccharide chains composed of repeating disaccharide units.  These units typically consist of an amino sugar (either N-acetylglucosamine or N-acetylgalactosamine) and a uronic acid (either glucuronic acid or iduronic acid). GAGs are major components of the extracellular matrix (ECM) and play crucial roles in various biological processes, including cell signaling, tissue hydration, and structural support.  Different types of GAGs exist, including hyaluronic acid, chondroitin sulfate, dermatan sulfate, keratan sulfate, and heparin. | The cartilage in our joints is rich in glycosaminoglycans, providing cushioning and lubrication. |近义词：mucopolysaccharides|反义词：无|
