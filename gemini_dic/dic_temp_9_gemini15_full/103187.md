jesuitical: /ˌdʒɛzjuˈɪtɪkəl/ | adj. | 耶稣会式的；狡猾的；诡辩的 |  Characterized by cleverness and trickery in speech or argument; cunning, especially in a way that seems morally questionable.  It often implies a deceptive use of logic or rhetoric to achieve one's aims.  The term is derived from the Jesuit order, a Catholic religious society, and historically carried negative connotations due to accusations of their sophisticated and sometimes manipulative rhetoric.

词源:

The word derives from the Society of Jesus (Jesuits), a Catholic religious order founded by Ignatius of Loyola in 1540. The Jesuits were known for their rigorous intellectual training and skilled debaters. Over time, the term "Jesuitical" acquired a negative connotation, associating their sophisticated arguments with manipulation and deception.
